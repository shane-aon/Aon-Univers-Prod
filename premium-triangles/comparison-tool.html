Gary is generally healthy, and only visits his physician once aside from his annual physical/wellness exam. His cost of care for the year is $220, because the annual physical is at no-cost.

If Gary chooses the Essential 20 or Advantage 10 Plan, Gary will spend nothing out of pocket, because he will be able to use the Galaxy contribution to his HSA to cover his medical costs. Gary will even have $280 remaining in his HSA for future health care costs. However, because of its low premiums, the HDHP Plan Plan is the lowest-cost plan in this scenario, even though Gary will have some Out of Pocket costs.

Gary has type 2 diabetes, a condition that requires regular doctor visits, tests and insulin treatment. The cost of his treatment adds $4,000 to his standard medical expenses.

Gary will pay out of pocket if he chooses the HDHP Plan, Advantage 10 or Essential 20 Plan, but because of the lower deductible and Out of Pocket maximum in the Essential 20 Plan, the Essential 20 Plan is the lowest-cost plan in this scenario.

Gary breaks his arm in a bike accident. Physician fees, x-rays, a cast and other items cost $1,200, which are in addition to his standard expenses.

The Advantage 10 Plan is the lowest cost option thanks to its premium savings compared to the Essential 20 Plan and the Galaxy Health Savings Account contribution, which he doesn’t get with the HDHP Plan Plan.

For an additional $166 in annual premium, Gary is protected from major accidental injuries.

Gary has a serious heart attack, and the cost above his standard expenses is very high – about $9,000 for this example.

Gary will pay out of pocket if he chooses the HDHP Plan, Advantage 10 or Essential 20 Plan, but because of the lower deductible and Out of Pocket maximum in the Essential 20 Plan, the Essential 20 Plan is the lowest-cost plan in this scenario.

Fortunately, Gary has $5,000 in critical illness coverage that he purchased for $8 per month (premium rates depend on age and tobacco status). The critical illness benefit is paid to him directly when he is diagnosed. It covers all of his costs and some is left over for incidental expenses.

They use medical services only when absolutely necessary and see their primary care physician once a year for routine sicknesses. Mike has high cholesterol and takes a generic statin.  Medical expenses for the year total $340.

Under the Essential 20 and Advantage 10 Plans, they pay nothing out of pocket, as their medical costs are covered by Galaxy contribution into their HSA. Even better, they save $660 that will stay in their HSA for future health needs. Because of its lower premium, the HDHP Plan Plan is the lowest-cost plan in this scenario.

Kelly has type 2 diabetes, a condition that requires regular doctor visits, tests and insulin treatment. Their total cost of medical care for the year is an additional $4,000 above their standard expenses. Because of its reasonable premium, the HDHP Plan Plan is the low cost option.

A fall off of a bike results in a broken arm for Mike. Physician fees, x-rays, a cast and other items cost $1,200, which are in addition to his standard expenses.

The HDHP Plan Plan Out of Pocket costs are the highest but the total cost of this plan is still the lowest because of the premium. The Essential 20 and Advantage 10 Plans have the lowest Out of Pocket costs due to the lower deductible and the HSA contribution.

If Mike and Kelly have accident insurance, their Out of Pocket costs are $0 under the two HSA plans and are reduced greatly under the HDHP Plan Plan.

Mike has a heart attack, and the cost above his standard expenses is very high – about $9,000 for this example. Along with the other routine family medical costs, their total cost of health care is lowest with the Advantage 10 Plan.

Fortunately, Mike and Kelly purchased $5,000 in critical illness for both of them. The critical illness benefit is paid to Mike directly when he is diagnosed. It covers all of his costs and some is left over for incidental expenses.

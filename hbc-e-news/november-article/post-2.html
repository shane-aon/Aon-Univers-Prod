Healthy Skin

Posted on November 2018

In late fall and winter your skin feels the effects of the cool, dry air, so it’s time to think about the health of your skin and how you can protect and maintain it. The American Academy of Dermatology (AAD) has designated November as National Healthy Skin Month, a time when everyone is encouraged to learn about the functions of the skin and how to keep it working and looking its best, not only during the winter, but all year round.


Learning about and protecting your skin is important. Changes in skin could be a sign that there is a problem with your overall health. Itching or a rash could indicate conditions such as an allergic reaction, an infection, or an autoimmune disease. A new growth or changing mole could turn out to be a type of skin cancer. Paying careful attention to your skin can provide important clues to the health and wellness of your whole body.


Here are just a few facts about your Skin:

  • The skin has three layers: epidermis, dermis, and subcutis.
  • The thickness of the skin varies between different parts of the body; skin is thickest on the palms and soles and thinnest on the eyelids.
  • The skin completely renews itself every 28 days by constantly shedding dead cells.
  • Skin that is damaged can heal itself by forming a scar. Unlike normal skin, scar tissue lacks hair and sweat glands.
  • Skin that is exposed to repeated friction or pressure can become thicker, forming a callus.
  • There are different types of receptors and nerve endings in the skin that respond to pressure, pain, and temperature.
  • The skin serves as a protective barrier that helps to keep water in the body and harmful chemicals and pathogens out.

SUGGESTIONS ON HOW TO KEEP YOUR SKIN HEALTHY


  1. Relax
    Manage your stress. Stress makes your skin break out and can make skin conditions like psoriasis much worse. Sleep between seven and nine hours a day. Exercise in the fresh air from time-to-time. Take deep breaths.

  2. Drink plenty of water to stay hydrated
    Drinking eight glasses of water daily is one way to moisturize your skin and clear out toxins that can harm skin. Lemons, cucumbers or other infusions can make your water refreshing.

  3. Check your skin for spots and blemishes
    Winter is an especially good time to check yourself carefully for spots with unusual shapes or colors that might indicate skin cancer or pre-cancerous conditions. Look for moles that seem to appear or change quickly. People with darker complexions aren't exempt from skin cancer. Although skin irregularities are not often apparent on darker skin, people of ethnic backgrounds tend to die more often from skin cancer.

  4. Apply sunscreen all year long
    Sunscreen is a powerful weapon against skin cancer, blocking ultraviolet rays that can do irreparable damage to your skin. Apply your sunscreen even during the winter months. It's recommended that you use an SPF 30 daily in any kind of weather.

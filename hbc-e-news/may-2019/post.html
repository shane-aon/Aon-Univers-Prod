MAY IS MENTAL HEALTH AWARENESS MONTH

Posted on May 2019

According to the World Health Organization, mental health is “a state of well-being in which the individual realizes his or her own abilities, can cope with the normal stresses of life, can work productively and fruitfully, and is able to contribute to his or her community….not merely the absence of disease….”1


You have likely seen the statistics:

  • In 2017, an estimated 46.6 million adults aged 18 or older in the United States live with a mental illness. This number represented 18.9% of all U.S. adults.
  • The prevalence of mental illness was higher among women (22.3%) than men (15.1%).
  • Young adults aged 18-25 years had the highest prevalence of mental illness (25.8%) compared to adults aged 26-49 years (22.2%) and aged 50 and older (13.8%).2

And, of this number, nearly one in four (4.5% of the general population) suffer from a mental, behavioral, or emotional disorder that substantially interferes with or limits one or more major life activities.2

So, if you have an average sized (3.14) family and group of friends (8.16), on average 2 of the individuals that mean the most to you live outside the definition of “mental health” and have a hard time coping with the normal stresses of life, work and society. The person impacted could be your mother, father, sibling, neighbor, friend, co-worker – or it could be you.


Mental Health Awareness helps us see mental health struggles as an illness that can often be managed by treatment, just like physical health conditions such as diabetes, blood pressure or cancer can be managed or treated. Awareness includes understanding that nearly all of us will need help with our mental health at some time – no one is immune. By eliminating the stigma of mental illness and mental health treatment we can improve access to care and treatment resources, and promote early intervention which can result in faster and more complete recovery from mental health issues.


So, what can you do?

Understand that mental health disorders affect you, your family, your friends and your co-workers. Be aware of the resources available to you through your community as well as those made available to you by HBC (see associated articles in this Wellness Newsletter). And work together to eliminate the negative adjectives that describe individuals with a mental illness, and the stigma associated with appropriate, timely treatment.

Together we can make a difference.

Beating the Winter Blues

Posted on Janaury 2019

Dark mornings, darker evenings, and chilly gray days in between mean winter is here — and with the coldest season come the winter blues. There’s no clinical diagnosis for the “winter blues,” but experts at the National Institutes of Health say the so-called winter blues are fairly common and are usually marked by feeling more down than usual, sad, or less energized.

Because the winter blues is not a discrete medical condition, an accurate measure of how many people it affects is difficult to know for sure. Estimates suggest anywhere from 14 to 20 percent of American adults experience such seasonal mood changes, and the further north you live, the higher the percentage becomes.

Here are some tips to help you get through this time of year:


  • Keep active
    A daily walk in the middle of the day can be very effective to cope with the winter blues. A brisk 10-minute walk counts for about a third of the recommended 30 minutes per day of moderate physical activity.

  • Get outside or let the outside in
    Go outdoors in natural daylight as much as possible, especially at midday and on brighter days. And if you can’t get outside, let the light in. Open your curtains, sit near windows.

  • Keep warm
    Being cold may make you feel more down, so staying warm may reduce the winter blues. Keep warm with hot drinks and hot food. Wear layers of warm clothes and wear warm shoes. When you are home and awake keep your home thermostat set between 68 and 72 degrees. (Setting the thermostat back by 7-10 degrees when you are sleeping or not at home can save up to 10% of your heating cost.)

  • Eat healthily
    A healthy diet will boost your mood, give you more energy and help you to not put on weight over winter. Balance your craving for carbohydrates, such as pasta and potatoes, with plenty of fresh fruit and vegetables.

  • Take up a new hobby
    Keep your mind active with a new interest to distract from the winter blues. Play cards, join a choir, or sewing club, or whatever your interest, join a gym, keep a journal, or write a blog. The important thing is that you have something to look forward to and concentrate on.

  • See your friends and family
    Social activity is good for your mental health and helps ward off the winter blues. Make a real effort to keep in touch with people you care about. Accept invitations to social events, even if you only go for a little while.

  • Talk through it
    Talk with your trusted friends and family members who are patient and accepting, or with your pastor, priest, rabbi, imam or other clergy.

  • Join a support group
    Think about joining a support group. Sharing your experience with others who know what it's like to have winter blues can be very therapeutic, and can make your symptoms more bearable.

COLORECTAL CANCER PREVENTION

Posted on April 2019

Roughly 140,000 people are diagnosed with colorectal cancer in the United States each year. It is the third most common cancer and No. 2 cause of cancer-related deaths among men and women. Colorectal cancer affects men and women of all racial and ethnic groups, and is most often found in people who are 50 years old or older.1


What Is Colorectal Cancer?

Colorectal cancer is cancer that occurs in the colon or rectum. Sometimes it is called colon cancer, for short. Sometimes abnormal growths, called polyps, form in the colon or rectum. Over time, some polyps may turn into cancer.


How is Colorectal Cancer detected?

Finding and treating colorectal cancer early may prevent death from colorectal cancer. Screening tests can find polyps so they can be removed before turning into cancer. Screening also helps find colorectal cancer at an early stage, when treatment works best. Colorectal surgeon Heidi Nelson, M.D., chair of surgery at Mayo Clinic, says “Early detection through screening is really important to increase survival odds. The survival rate is roughly 90 percent when it is caught early.2” There are now multiple screening options, including colonoscopies; a stool DNA test, a virtual colonoscopy using a CT scan; and more.

The problem is, when you have symptoms of colorectal cancer, it may be too late. Early and regular screening is important. The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force recommends that adults age 50 to 75 should get appropriate tests. These tests include:


  • Stool tests for the presence of blood – once a year
  • Flexible Sigmoidoscopy (lower colon) – every 5 years
  • Colonoscopy (entire colon) – every 10 years (for people who do not have an increased risk)
  • CT Colonography – every 5 years.

Each of these test has pros and cons, and should be conducted on the advice of your physician.


How can Colorectal Cancer be prevented?

Avoiding cancer risk factors may help prevent certain cancers. There are some risks you cannot change, like Age, Family History of colorectal cancer, Personal History of polyps or bowel deseases or hereditary gene changes and Race. However, there are several risks you can control, including:


  • Alcohol use - Drinking 3 or more alcoholic beverages per day increases the risk of colorectal cancer. Limit drinks to 1-2 per day and, if you don’t drink, don’t start.;
  • Cigarette smoking is linked to an increased risk of colorectal cancer and death from colorectal cancer. Smoking cigarettes is also linked to an increased risk of forming benign tumors. The sooner you quit, the sooner your risk begins to decrease.;
  • Overweight and Obesity increases the risk of colorectal cancer.

Plus, there are several protective factors that can decrease the risk of colorectal cancer, including:



  • Diet. The CDC recommends a diet low in animal fats and high in fruits, vegetables and whole grains.
  • Exercise. A lifestyle that includes regular physical activity is linked to a decreased risk of colorectal cancer. A minimum 30 minutes of moderate activity per day for 5 days each week is recommended by the CDC.
  • Aspirin. The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force found that taking low-dose aspirin lowers the risk of colorectal cancer and cardiovascular disease.
